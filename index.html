My story

I lived my early years as an awkward child in a small town in Warwickshire. I was massively disappointed when I failed my 11+ exam and had to go to the local High School. Despite being bullied I poured myself into my work and got all A grades in my GCSEs. With my confidence boosted I went to another local school to study my A-levels and took a chance on an application to the University of Oxford. I worked hard for the entrance exams and was delighted when my application to read Engineering, Economics, & Management (EEM) was accepted.

Engineering at Oxford was hard. At the time we did lots of mathematics and surprising little computer based work. The parts of the course I most enjoyed were the management and digital logic papers.

I started my working life as a management consultant in the financial services industry. I soon found myself drawn to improving business processes and creating complex Excel spreadsheets. This is where I got my first real taste for coding: writing Visual Basic to make my spreadsheets more powerful. Though I enjoyed the varied nature of my job I wasn't excited about the industry. I was often stressed and found myself heading for an emotional breakdown. During this time I had my first experience of psychotherapy and shifted my attention from modelling business finances to modelling the human experience.

I did lots of courses and became a qualified psychotherapist working privately from my home therapy room. I am deeply interested in the field and I'm working towards being able to articulate a novel perspective on the structure of experience. I enjoy the art and science of 'de-programming' people and think I'm quite good at it. However, it is a rather solitary pursuit and I find the marketing side of the business is a real grind.

In future I'd like to have a day job that gets me out of the house, working with bright people, solving problems, and creating something new that helps people. I think software development could well fit the bill. I'd like to continue to practice psychotherapy but reduce my committment to a couple of evenings a week or, perhaps, every other weekend.
